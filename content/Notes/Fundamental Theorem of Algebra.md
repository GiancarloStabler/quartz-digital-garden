#theorem #abstract-algebra 

#### Intuition
We know this from as early as pre-algebra! And note that this doesn't hold for general cases in $\mathbb{R}$.

#### Formal Statement
Every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has a zero in $\mathbb{C}$. 

#### Corollaries
If $p \in P(\mathbb{C})$ is a non-constant polynomial, then $p$ has a unique factorization (up to order) of the form,
$$
p(z) =c(z-\lambda_{1})\cdots(z-\lambda_{m}),
$$
where $c,\lambda_{1},\ldots, \lambda_{m}$ are complex numbers.
